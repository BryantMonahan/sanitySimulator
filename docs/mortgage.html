Mortgages

What is a Mortgage?

A mortgage is a loan that is used to purchase a home. The loan is secured by the home itself, so if the borrower fails to repay the loan, the lender can take possession of the home through a process called foreclosure. Mortgages are typically repaid over a period of 15 to 30 years, with fixed or adjustable interest rates.

How Do Mortgages Work?

When a borrower applies for a mortgage, the lender will evaluate their creditworthiness, income, and other financial factors to determine how much they can borrow and at what interest rate. The borrower will then make a down payment, which is a percentage of the home's purchase price, and the lender will provide the remaining funds needed to buy the home. The borrower will then make monthly payments to the lender, which will include both principal and interest, until the loan is paid off.

Types of Mortgages

There are several types of mortgages available. The most common type of loan is a fixed mortgage and will be the contents of this page will be most applicable to this type of loan. However, there are other types of loans that are available to borrowers, the ones listed are just the most common.

  • Fixed-rate mortgage: A mortgage with a fixed interest rate for the entire term of the loan.

  • Adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM): A mortgage with an interest rate that can change periodically based on market conditions.

  • FHA loan: A government-backed mortgage designed for low-to-moderate-income borrowers.

  • VA loan: A government-backed mortgage available to eligible veterans and active-duty service members.

  • Jumbo loan: A non-conforming loan that exceeds the limits set by the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA), typically used for financing high-value properties.

Why You Should Care

The 30-year fixed mortgage rate plays a critical role in home affordability, directly affecting the total cost of borrowing for homeowners. Even a small change in the interest rate can have a significant financial impact over the life of a loan.

For example, on a $500,000 mortgage, an increase from 5% to 6% raises the monthly payment by approximately $323 and results in nearly $116,000 more in interest paid over 30 years. This means that even a 1% increase in rates can substantially raise the long-term cost of homeownership.

Higher mortgage rates reduce purchasing power, making homes less affordable and slowing demand in the housing market, while lower rates encourage homebuying and refinancing, boosting economic activity. This sensitivity to interest rates is why they are closely monitored by homebuyers, real estate professionals, and policymakers.

Mortgage rates experienced a sharp increase in the late 1970s, peaking at nearly 18%. After this peak, rates declined steadily over the following decades, reaching historic lows around 2020. However, following this period, mortgage rates rose significantly.
